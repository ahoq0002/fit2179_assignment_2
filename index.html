From Sporting Nation
to Sedentary Nation?

Australia has long defined itself through sport — from backyard cricket to Olympic gold, the active life is woven into the national identity. Yet beneath this image, the data tells a more complicated story.

Using two landmark Australian Bureau of Statistics datasets — the 2013–14 Participation in Sport and Physical Recreation survey and the 2022 Physical Activity report — this visualisation traces who is active, what they do, and who is being left behind. Participation differs dramatically by state, age, gender and socioeconomic status, raising important questions about equity, infrastructure and public health.

1The Geographic Picture

Where is Australia
Most Active?

Physical activity participation varies considerably across Australia's states and territories. The ACT and Tasmania lead the nation, while Queensland records the lowest overall rate. Age shapes participation just as strongly as geography.

66.1% ACT — highest state participation rate (2013–14)
59.1% Queensland — lowest participation rate nationally
7 pts Typical gap between male and female participation rates
72.1% Participation rate among 15–17 year olds — the most active group

Physical Activity Participation Rate by State & Territory

Darker shading indicates higher participation. Hover over any state for a breakdown by sex. The ACT and Tasmania consistently outperform other jurisdictions, likely reflecting higher proportions of public-sector workers and university populations with access to facilities and flexible working arrangements.

★ The ACT (66.1%) and Tasmania (65.2%) lead the nation in participation, while Queensland (59.1%) sits 7 percentage points below the capital territory. Across all states, male participation rates exceed female by 6–8 points.

Every state shows a consistent male advantage. The gap is largest in the Northern Territory and smallest in Tasmania.

State Participation Heat Map

Colour intensity encodes participation rate across three measures. The ACT and Tasmania row glow the brightest across all columns, while Queensland remains lower across all three measures.

★ Participation patterns remain geographically uneven across Australia.

Line Chart · Age Groups

How Participation Declines with Age

Physical activity rates peak in the 15–17 age group and decline progressively through adulthood. Click on a line to isolate it. The gender gap narrows slightly in older cohorts, but the downward trend is consistent for both sexes — highlighting ageing as a key challenge for national participation strategies.

Participation drops from 72% among teenagers to just 40% for those aged 75+. The most dramatic decline occurs between ages 17 and 35 — a window for targeted intervention.

2Activity Preferences

What Do Australians
Actually Do?

Walking dominates Australian participation data by a wide margin, followed by fitness activities and swimming. But the choice of sport is deeply gendered — with men favouring competitive and outdoor pursuits, while women lead in fitness and walking activities.

Ranked Bar Chart · Top 15 Activities

Australia's Most Popular Physical Activities

Total participants (in thousands) across the top 15 activities, coloured by category. Click or hover for a tooltip showing the activity's share of total participation. Click on legend to isolate categories. Walking for exercise dominates with over 6.4 million participants — more than any team sport combined.

Walking (6.45M), Aerobics/Fitness (3.9M) and Swimming (3.47M) form Australia's "big three" — activities that require minimal equipment, are low-impact, and accessible across all age groups.

Diverging Bar Chart · Gender Gap

Which Activities Show the Biggest Gender Divide?

Bars extending left show male dominance; bars extending right show female dominance. Activities are sorted by female participation rate. Walking and aerobics are strongly female-dominated, while cycling and golf skew heavily male.

Walking shows the largest female dominance (65.8% vs 35.2%), while golf has the largest male skew (20.2% vs 6.1%). These differences reflect broader social norms around recreation and competitive sport.

★ Gender gaps are not uniform across sport: lifestyle and fitness activities tend to attract more female participation, while traditional competitive team sports and cycling remain more male-skewed.

3Organised Sport

Beyond the Playing Field —
Community Sport's Engine

Organised sport relies on far more than players. Coaches, referees, administrators and volunteers make up a large and underappreciated share of Australia's sporting ecosystem — and the vast majority serve without pay.

~80% Non-playing role holders who serve as unpaid volunteers
NSW Largest absolute number of organised sport participants nationally
3:1 Approximate ratio of male to female players in organised sport

100% Normalised Stacked Bar · Role Composition by State

How Australians Are Involved in Organised Sport by State

Each bar totals 100% of organised sport involvement in that state, split by role type. Inline labels show the share for larger segments. States with a higher share of non-playing roles tend to reflect more developed volunteer networks and club infrastructure.

★ The ACT and Victoria show notably higher proportions of combined playing/non-playing involvement, suggesting more integrated club structures. Playing-only roles dominate nationally, but non-playing roles are substantial in every state.

Proportional Symbol Map · Volunteer vs Paid Involvement

Volunteer Involvement Dwarfs Paid Participation Across Every State

Each teal circle is sized proportionally to the number of unpaid volunteers in that state. The small amber dot inside each circle represents paid involvement. Hover any marker for the exact figures. The sheer size difference reveals how thoroughly community sport depends on unpaid labour.

★ NSW alone accounts for over 740,000 volunteer sport participants — more than 12 times the number with any paid involvement. Victoria follows closely. Without this volunteer workforce, organised sport in Australia would collapse.

Radial Bar Chart · Volunteer Dependence Ratio

Every State Relies Heavily on Unpaid Sport Labour

Each radial bar shows how many unpaid volunteers exist for every one paid sport participant in that state. Longer bars mean greater dependence on community labour. Every state exceeds 8 volunteers per paid participant.

★ In every state and territory, unpaid volunteers outnumber paid sport workers by at least 8 to 1 — revealing how deeply organised sport depends on community labour.

★ Community sport across Australia is sustained far more by civic contribution than by paid employment.

4Modern Inactivity

The Quiet Crisis of
Australian Inactivity

Fast-forward to 2022 and new data from the ABS reveals a sobering picture. Very few Australians meet their own government's physical activity guidelines. Inactivity is sharply tied to socioeconomic disadvantage — and sedentary work is now the norm for most working Australians.

17.2% Adults in the most disadvantaged quintile who did zero activity last week (2022)
7.8% Adults in the least disadvantaged quintile — the inactivity gap is real
31.3% Only about 1 in 3 adults aged 18–24 meet full physical activity guidelines
54.4% Adults aged 35–44 who mostly sit at work — a striking majority

Dot Plot with 95% Confidence Intervals · Socioeconomic Inequality

Inactivity by Socioeconomic Group — Zero Minutes of Activity in the Past Week

Each dot represents the proportion of adults in that disadvantage quintile who did zero minutes of physical activity in the week prior to the 2022 ABS survey. Horizontal lines show the 95% confidence interval. The colour gradient — green to red — visually encodes the inequality gradient from most to least advantaged. The shaded zone marks the elevated-concern threshold above 14%.

★ The most disadvantaged Australians are more than twice as likely to be completely inactive as the most advantaged. This gap — 17.2% vs 7.8% — confirms that physical inactivity is not simply a personal choice but a structural consequence of socioeconomic conditions.

Parallel Coordinates · Guideline Measures Across Age Groups

How Activity Measures Diverge — Meeting Full Guidelines vs Doing Some Activity

Each line traces one guideline metric across age groups. Background lines (muted) show partial activity measures per Week — many Australians achieve these. The bold red line — Met Full Guidelines — runs far below all others, demonstrating how few Australians satisfy the complete aerobic + strength training combination across every age cohort.

★ Even at peak fitness ages (18–24), barely one in three Australians meets full guidelines. The red line's persistent separation from the others tells the core story: doing some exercise and meeting guidelines are very different things.

Normalized Stacked Area Chart · Work Activity Composition

Work Activity Type Shifts Dramatically Across the Life Course

Each band represents the share of workers whose jobs are primarily sitting, standing, walking, or physically demanding across different age groups. The chart reveals a strong rise in sedentary desk-based work through prime working ages, while physically active roles become less common. Percentages are normalized to show how the composition of occupational activity changes across the workforce life course.

★ Desk-based work peaks during ages 25–54, reinforcing the challenge of achieving meaningful physical activity outside work hours for Australia's largest working cohorts.

Conclusion

The Active Life Is Not
Equally Available to All

Australia's identity as a sporting nation is real — but it rests on a more fragile and unequal foundation than the national mythology suggests.

📉

Participation falls with age

Physical activity rates peak in adolescence and decline steadily through adulthood, with the steepest drop occurring between ages 17 and 35. By the time Australians reach their 70s, participation has fallen to barely half its teenage peak — underscoring the need for age-appropriate pathways that extend beyond youth sport.

⚖️

Inequality shapes inactivity

Adults in the most socioeconomically disadvantaged communities are more than twice as likely to do zero physical activity as their most advantaged counterparts. This is not a story of individual motivation — it reflects structural barriers including access to facilities, safe outdoor spaces, leisure time, and financial resources.

🪑

Work has become sedentary

For most working-age Australians, the majority of the day is now spent sitting. Sedentary work dominates ages 25–54 — precisely the life stage when meeting physical activity guidelines becomes hardest and most consequential for long-term health. Leisure-time activity must compensate for what the workplace no longer provides.

🤝

Organised sport runs on volunteers

Behind every game played in an organised competition stands a network of unpaid coaches, administrators, and referees. Across every state and territory, unpaid volunteers outnumber paid sport workers by 8 to 12 times. This volunteer workforce is invisible in most public accounts of sport — yet without it, community sport as Australians know it would not exist.

🎯

Guidelines are rarely met

Even among the most active age group — 18 to 24 year olds — fewer than one in three Australians satisfy the full national physical activity guidelines. The gap between "doing some exercise" and "meeting the standard" is vast, suggesting that current approaches to promoting activity are reaching people partially, but not deeply enough.

🏅

Gender gaps persist across roles

Male participation outpaces female across almost every sport and organised role, with the largest gaps concentrated in competitive and outdoor activities. The exception is walking and fitness — activities that are more accessible, lower cost, and less socially gatekept. Closing the gender gap in sport requires addressing the structural and cultural conditions that shape who gets to play.

Sport and physical activity are not luxuries. They shape mental health, social connection, community identity, and quality of life across the lifespan. The data in this visualisation does not tell a story of failure — it tells a story of potential: a nation that is broadly active, deeply invested in community sport, and capable of closing the gaps that inequality, age, and occupational change have opened up.

The question is not whether Australians value physical activity. Clearly, they do. The question is whether the systems, spaces, and support structures around them make it equally possible for all of them to act on that value.
